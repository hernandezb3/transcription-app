The Big Picture

Overview

When you upload an audio file, the system needs to answer three questions:

  • What was said? → Speech-to-text transcription
  • When was it said? → Timestamps for each word and sentence
  • Who said it? → Speaker identification (diarization)

These are handled by separate AI models that each specialize in one job, then their results are combined at the end.

Analogy: Think of it like a courtroom. One person (the stenographer) types what's being said. A separate person (the clerk) keeps track of who's speaking. At the end, you merge the stenographer's transcript with the clerk's speaker log.

Step 1 — Transcribe the Audio

Speech → Text
🧠
NVIDIA Parakeet TDT 0.6B
A 600-million-parameter speech recognition model. It listens to the audio and produces text along with timestamps for every word and sentence.

What it does:

  • Takes the raw audio waveform as input
  • Converts sound into a sequence of characters and words
  • Records the exact start and end time of each word
  • Groups words into natural sentences (segments)
Output example:
"I don't even have a car." → starts at 0.32s, ends at 1.76s
"How did I get a parking ticket?" → starts at 1.84s, ends at 3.68s

At this point the system knows what was said and when, but has no idea who said it. Everything is attributed to one unnamed speaker.

Step 2 — Detect When Someone Is Speaking

Voice Activity Detection
🎚️
MarbleNet (VAD)
A small neural network that scans the audio and marks which time ranges contain human speech versus silence, music, or background noise.

What it does:

  • Slides a small window across the entire audio file
  • At each position, decides: "Is someone talking here? Yes or no."
  • Outputs a list of speech regions: "speech from 0.1s–3.9s, silence from 3.9s–4.0s, speech from 4.0s–7.5s …"
Analogy: This is like a light sensor at the door of a meeting room. It doesn't know who walked in — it just records when the door opened and closed.

The speech regions are then chopped into smaller overlapping windows that get passed to the next step.

Step 3 — Create Voice Fingerprints

Speaker Embeddings
🔬
TitaNet Large
NVIDIA's speaker verification model. It listens to a short clip of audio and produces a compact numerical "fingerprint" of the voice — a 192-number vector that captures what makes that voice unique.

What it does:

  • Takes each speech segment and processes it at 5 different time scales (0.5s, 0.75s, 1.0s, 1.25s, and 1.5s windows)
  • For each window, produces a 192-dimensional vector — the voice fingerprint
  • Two clips of the same person will produce similar vectors
  • Two clips of different people will produce very different vectors
Analogy: Imagine you could distill someone's voice into a unique barcode. Even if they say different words, their barcode stays roughly the same. A different person gets a different barcode.
Multiple window sizes are used because a 0.5-second clip captures quick speaker changes, while a 1.5-second clip captures more stable voice characteristics. Using all five together gives the best of both worlds.

Step 4 — Group Voices Into Speakers

Spectral Clustering

Now we have hundreds of voice fingerprints from across the audio. The system needs to figure out: "How many distinct people are there, and which fingerprints belong to the same person?"

How it figures out the number of speakers:

  1. Compare every fingerprint to every other fingerprint. This creates a big grid (similarity matrix) where each cell says "how similar do these two clips sound?"
  2. Build a graph where similar fingerprints are connected by strong edges and dissimilar ones by weak edges.
  3. Analyze the graph's structure using a mathematical technique called eigenvalue decomposition. This reveals natural groupings.
  4. Look for the "eigengap" — a big jump in the math that signals where one group of voices ends and another begins. The number of groups before the jump = the number of speakers.
Analogy: Imagine you have 200 photos of faces but no labels. You lay them all out on a table and start grouping by similarity. You'd naturally end up with a few piles — one per person. That's essentially what clustering does, but with voice fingerprints instead of photos.

Once the number of speakers is known:

  • Each fingerprint is assigned to a group: Speaker 0, Speaker 1, Speaker 2, etc.
  • Results from all 5 time scales are merged by majority vote
  • A safety cap (max_num_speakers) prevents the system from hallucinating too many speakers in noisy audio
Important: The speaker labels are anonymous — "speaker_0" just means "the first distinct voice detected." The system doesn't know anyone's name. It only knows that certain audio segments sound like the same person.

Step 5 — Combine Everything

Final Assembly

At this point we have two independent results:

Transcription (Step 1)
→
"I don't even have a car." at 0.32s–1.76s
Diarization (Steps 2–4)
→
speaker_2 is talking from 0.10s–3.90s

The merge process:

  1. For each transcribed sentence, check which speaker's time range overlaps the most
  2. Assign that speaker label to the sentence
  3. If consecutive sentences have the same speaker, combine them into one block

Final output:

speaker_2: I don't even have a car. How did I get a parking ticket? [0.32s – 3.68s]
speaker_0: I think onions are a really great vegetable. They're really healthy for you. [4.00s – 7.44s]
speaker_1: Our voice recorder is a convenient and simple online tool that can be used right in your browser. [7.84s – 13.04s]
speaker_2: Privacy guaranteed. We guarantee that our app is secure. Everything you record is accessible to you alone. [15.68s – 23.12s]
speaker_0: If I could have one thing in my life, it'd probably be cheese pizza. [23.68s – 27.52s]
speaker_1: Free to use. Voice recorder is completely free. No hidden payments, activation fees, or charges for extra features. [28.80s – 34.88s]

Limitations to Be Aware Of

Important
  • Speaker labels are anonymous. The system outputs "speaker_0", "speaker_1", etc. It cannot determine real names without pre-enrolled voice samples.
  • Overlapping speech is hard. If two people talk at the same time, the system may attribute the text to whichever speaker had more overlap.
  • Audio quality matters. Background noise, echo, or low-quality microphones can cause the system to split one person into multiple speakers or merge two people into one.
  • Speaker count is estimated, not guaranteed. The eigengap method is reliable but not perfect. For legal or medical use, speaker assignments should always be reviewed by a human.
  • Processing time. Transcription and diarization both take time — a 30-minute audio file may take several minutes to process, depending on hardware.
All models are from NVIDIA NeMo — open-source and run locally. No audio is sent to external servers.
